\chapter{Literature Survey} 
\label{section:lit_survey}
Object detection and multi-object tracking (MOT) are core components of modern video analytics systems used in surveillance, 
traffic monitoring, and autonomous perception. In such systems, detection estimates object locations in each frame, while 
tracking enforces temporal consistency and identity assignment across frames. The quality of an MOT pipeline is therefore 
jointly determined by detector robustness, data association strategy, and computational efficiency 
\cite{Milan2016MOT16, Bewley2016_sort}. This chapter reviews relevant literature with emphasis on methods that are compatible 
with real-time and hardware-oriented deployment.

\section{Related Work}
\label{section:related_work}

\subsection{Evolution of Object Detection}
Early object detection methods used hand-crafted features and classical machine learning. Viola and Jones proposed a 
cascade framework using Haar-like features for rapid face detection \cite{ViolaJones2001}. Dalal and Triggs later demonstrated 
the effectiveness of Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) for pedestrian detection \cite{DalalTriggs2005}. While these 
methods were computationally efficient for their time, they were limited in generalization across object categories and 
complex scenes.

The introduction of deep convolutional neural networks significantly improved detection accuracy. Region-based approaches such as 
Faster R-CNN \cite{Ren2015FasterRCNN} offered strong accuracy by combining region proposal mechanisms with deep feature extraction, 
but incurred relatively high inference latency. In contrast, one-stage detectors such as YOLO reframed detection as a 
single regression problem, enabling real-time throughput \cite{Redmon2016YOLO}. Successive variants 
(for example, YOLOv3 and YOLOv4) improved multi-scale feature representation and training strategies, improving the 
speed-accuracy trade-off \cite{RedmonFarhadi2018YOLOv3, Bochkovskiy2020YOLOv4}. Recent practical implementations 
such as Ultralytics YOLOv8 continue this trend by providing efficient inference and deployment-friendly tooling 
\cite{Jocher2023Ultralytics}.

\subsection{Multi-Object Tracking and Data Association}
MOT aims to maintain consistent trajectories and identities over time despite missed detections, clutter, and occlusion. 
A common tracking-by-detection formulation separates the problem into three stages: state prediction, association, and update. 
The Kalman filter \cite{Kalman1960} remains a standard estimator for linear motion models due to its low complexity and recursive design. 
For assignment, the Hungarian algorithm provides an optimal bipartite matching solution in polynomial 
time \cite{Kuhn1955Hungarian}.

Benchmarking efforts such as MOTChallenge have standardized metrics and datasets for comparing trackers \cite{Milan2016MOT16}. 
These benchmarks also highlight that tracking accuracy is highly dependent on detector quality and association robustness, 
particularly under dense scenes and partial occlusions.

\subsection{SORT and Its Extensions}
Simple Online and Realtime Tracking (SORT) is a lightweight baseline that combines Kalman filtering with IoU-based association 
and Hungarian matching \cite{Bewley2016_sort}. SORT is attractive for embedded and real-time systems 
because it uses minimal appearance modeling and low computational overhead. However, its reliance on geometric 
overlap and short-term motion assumptions makes it sensitive to long occlusions and abrupt motion.

To address identity switches in crowded scenes, DeepSORT adds appearance descriptors to the association stage, 
improving re-identification performance \cite{Wojke2017DeepSORT}. More recent trackers such as ByteTrack revisit association 
heuristics by including low-confidence detections to recover difficult trajectories \cite{Zhang2022ByteTrack}. 
These approaches improve robustness but typically increase computational and implementation complexity compared to 
baseline SORT.

\subsection{Hardware and Real-Time Implementation Considerations}
For practical deployments, latency, memory bandwidth, and power constraints are often as important as benchmark accuracy. 
Surveys on efficient deep learning indicate that algorithm-hardware co-design is essential for edge systems, 
especially when combining neural inference with tracking logic \cite{Sze2017EfficientDNN}. In this context, SORT-style 
pipelines are advantageous because major operations (state prediction, covariance update, and gating) are structured and 
arithmetic-heavy, making them suitable for fixed-point and hardware implementations.

\subsection{Research Gap and Project Positioning}
From the reviewed literature, three observations motivate this project:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item High-accuracy detectors exist, but end-to-end MOT deployment on constrained platforms remains challenging.
	\item Robust trackers with appearance models improve identity preservation but increase computational cost.
	\item Lightweight trackers such as SORT offer a strong baseline for real-time and hardware-oriented realization.
\end{enumerate}

Accordingly, this work focuses on a practical detector-plus-SORT pipeline, and explores how core tracking operations can be 
mapped toward hardware-friendly modules while preserving online behavior and low latency.

\section{Tools and Technologies}
\label{section:tools_tech}

\subsection{Detection Backbone}
The detection front-end is based on YOLO family principles, selected for its real-time characteristics and mature 
tooling ecosystem \cite{Redmon2016YOLO, Jocher2023Ultralytics}. The literature consistently shows that stronger 
detections directly improve downstream tracking quality, particularly for online trackers that do not rely heavily on 
re-identification features \cite{Bewley2016_sort}.

\subsection{Tracking Core}
The tracking core follows the SORT design: a linear Kalman filter for state estimation, IoU-based gating for feasible matches, 
and Hungarian assignment for one-to-one pairing between predictions and 
detections \cite{Kalman1960, Kuhn1955Hungarian, Bewley2016_sort}. This combination is well-supported in literature as 
a strong real-time baseline for MOT.

\subsection{Software Prototyping Stack}
Python-based prototyping with computer vision libraries is widely adopted in prior work for rapid algorithm validation. 
OpenCV provides stable, optimized primitives for frame I/O, geometry operations, and 
visualization \cite{Bradski2000OpenCV}. This enables quick experimentation with detector outputs and tracker parameter 
tuning before hardware migration.

\subsection{Hardware Mapping Perspective}
Literature on efficient inference and edge deployment motivates moving deterministic arithmetic blocks to 
hardware \cite{Sze2017EfficientDNN}. In this project context, matrix and vector operations in prediction and update stages 
are analyzed for resource-aware implementation, while preserving compatibility with streaming detector outputs.

\subsection{Summary of Survey}
The literature supports a staged approach: use a strong real-time detector, pair it with a lightweight online 
tracker, and optimize arithmetic-heavy components for hardware execution. This directly informs the methodology of the 
present work, where algorithmic clarity and deployment feasibility are treated as co-equal goals.
